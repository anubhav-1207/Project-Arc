# Arc As A School Project

I didn't start Arc just as a programming language. I started it because I wanted to be different.

When project time came in high school, everyone built the same things like library management systems, games, todo apps.Which were safe but forgettable. I wanted to build something that meant something. Something that would force me to understand how software actually works at its core. So I decided to build a language which was something that would last.

I had about six to eight months which was the deadline. That was all the time the school gave me to learn compiler theory, design a language, and build an interpreter from scratch. No room for error. No extensions.If I had doubts,I was all alone.

The constraints were brutal. I didn't have a desktop or laptop. I coded everything on an Android tablet from 2021 running Linux through Termux. The hardware was against me from the start. It has 4GB of RAM,a 10-inch screen, a processor that barely kept up, a keyboard and mouse that connected maybe less than half the time when it felt like it. But I didn't choose this because I wanted to suffer but rather I chose it because my family couldn't afford anything better. I've been coding since I was 12 on a phone, typing every line on my screen. So a tablet and a keyboard felt like some real upgrade at the time. 

Arc started when I was 16. By then, there was almost no support at home. My dad passed away a year ago when I was 15. Money didn't exist and my mom hated that I coded. She thought it was worthless, a waste of time. If I spent more than an hour on it, she'd scold me and tell me to stop. But I didn't stop or rather I'd say I couldn't stop. Because loving something when no one around you understands it, when everything and everyone tells you it's pointless,that's when you know it's real.

Everyone said the same thing: don't waste your time. You'll learn it in university anyway. As if the future could erase the present. As if passion has an expiration date.

I had no one to ask. Everyone around me was too ordinary. They didn't care how computers worked. They didn't want to know. They just used them. So when I hit walls, and I hit many, there was no one. No mentor, no peer, no voice to guide me through the darkness. Just me, my 2021 tablet and the questions I couldn't answer.

Learning resources were a luxury I didn't have because books costed money I didn't have and tutorials were either paywalled, or used resources my tablet couldn't handle. I was alone with a dying tablet and questions that no one could answer. So I turned to Claude. It became my only teacher. My only voice. I owe it everything. But understand this: I didn't copy code. I learned. I struggled. I built Arc myself,every function, every decision, every line. It's mine.

It cost me a lot, some friendships faded because I chose code over hangouts. Some family relations strained because I wouldn't let go of this obsession. People thought I was wasting my life. Maybe they were right. But Arc was mine. It represented me, the part of me that refused to accept the easy path, that wouldn't settle for what everyone else was doing. It was stubborn. It was hungry. It was real.

This is Arc. This is why it exists. This is what it cost.
